Your governments

Enter a California address and the Ledger shows the governments that spend in your name — your city (or, outside any city, your county), your county, and the state — as separate records. They are different accounting bases, and money moves between them, so this page never adds them into a total. It describes spending on residents' behalf, not anyone's tax bill.

    Where the address goes: to locate it, the address — including as-you-type candidate lookups once you have typed a house number — is sent to the U.S. Census Bureau's public geocoder (census.gov) — they receive the address and your network address, as with any federal website, and set a load-balancer cookie. It is sent nowhere else, is never stored by this page, and never appears in permalinks, citations, or CSV files — those carry only the resolved city and county, facts shared with everyone who lives there. Prefer not to type an address? The pin option sends only map coordinates; after a lookup failure you can also resolve entirely on this device.

    Methodology

    This page joins the Ledger's reconciled layers for one location. Its rules exist because the easy version — one big total — would be wrong by construction.

    METHOD NOTES
    A-1The figures do not add, and this page never adds them. Money moves between the layers: in the most recent fiscal year, of California counties' governmental revenues came from the state and federal governments — dollars that also appear in those governments' own figures (). The state's enacted budget likewise includes money sent to local governments. A single stacked total would count the same dollars more than once, so no total exists anywhere on this page. The shares above are computed by the county pipeline from the Controller's revenue data on every run — this page hardcodes none of them.
    A-2"In your name" — never a bill. Per-resident figures divide a government's spending by the population it reports serving. They are not a tax bill: renters, commuters, visitors, and businesses all contribute to these budgets, and no honest per-address cost exists. This page describes spending on residents' behalf and refuses the other framing.
    A-3Where the address goes. The address (or pin coordinates) is sent to the U.S. Census Bureau's public geocoder over HTTPS via its supported JSONP interface, and nowhere else — this site has no server. Census.gov receives the query and your network address and sets a load-balancer cookie. Nothing is stored on this page; permalinks, citations, and CSV files carry only resolved city/county identifiers. The "resolve on this device" option sends nothing anywhere and matches the point against the simplified boundaries this site already ships — near a city limit that simplification can be wrong, which is why it is the fallback, not the default.
    A-4Jurisdiction assignment is the Census Bureau's, not ours. City and county come from the geocoder's full-resolution TIGER geography (county, incorporated place, and Census-designated place), not from this site's simplified map shapes. An address outside every incorporated city is shown for what it is: the county is its local government. A Census-designated place, when present, is shown as community context only — a statistical area, not a government.
    A-5Special districts are not assigned to addresses. No statewide special-district boundary file exists, and the property-tax system's district lists cannot be joined honestly to the Controller's filings (see docs/V6_ADDRESS_FINDING.md). This page states how many districts file from the county and links to their records; it never claims to know which serve a specific address.
    A-6Bases and vintages are labeled per record. City and county figures are reported actuals, reconciled against the Controller's published control totals. The state figure is the enacted budget — a plan, on the Budgetary-Legal basis (the state page's Actuals view shows how enacted years compared with published actuals). Rural address matching in the Census benchmark is imperfect; when the geocoder finds no match, this page says so and offers the pin, the on-device option, and the regular search pickers — it never guesses.
    SOURCES & RECORD
    Geocoding: U.S. Census Bureau Geocoder (geocoding.geo.census.gov, Public_AR_Current benchmark, Current vintage). Figures: the same data files as the rest of the Ledger — data.js, city-data.js, county-data.js, district-data.js — each SHA-256 digest-stamped and verifiable with pipeline/verify_digest.py. This page computes nothing new; it re-presents the layers side by side for one location.
