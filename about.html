THE PUBLIC RECORD · ABOUT & METHOD

What this is, and how it is checked

Citizen Ledger is a nonpartisan record of California government spending: the state's enacted budget and actual expenditures, and the reported finances of its cities, counties, special districts, and K-12 schools — in one place, on one set of rules. It is built for anyone who needs the numbers: residents, journalists, researchers, and officials use the same pages, the same figures, and the same tools. Nothing here is behind an account, and nothing is tailored to an audience.

New here? Reading the Ledger is a plain-language guide to what the record is, why the layers do not add up, what the gate tiers mean, and why “not published” is not zero. This page is the method reference underneath it.

The record, layer by layer

Every figure on this site comes from an official state source, named below with its accounting basis — what kind of number it is — and how often it updates. The bases differ across layers, each page says which basis it is on, and figures on different bases are never mixed or added.

LAYERSOURCEACCOUNTING BASISUPDATES
State budget The Department of Finance's eBudget publication (ebudget.ca.gov) — the data behind the state's own Enacted Budget pages, to the department, fund, and program. Enacted appropriations under California's Budgetary-Legal basis — a spending plan, fixed when each year's Budget Act is signed. One new fiscal year per Budget Act, typically late June.
State actuals The Department of Finance's Schedule 9 — the state's own comparative statement of what was actually spent, extracted from the Governor's Budget publications. Actual expenditures, Budgetary-Legal basis — the same basis as the enacted figures they sit beside, unaudited by the Ledger. Actuals for a fiscal year first appear about six and a half months after it ends, in the following January's Governor's Budget.
Cities & counties The State Controller's "By the Numbers" portal (bythenumbers.sco.ca.gov) — the standardized annual financial reports all 482 cities and 57 counties file, to the state form line. Reported actual revenues and expenditures — retrospective figures as filed, not budgets. Annually; a fiscal year's reports publish roughly a year after it closes.
K-12 schools The California Department of Education's SACS unaudited actuals — the general ledgers of every school district, county office, and charter school, plus CDE's published Current Expense of Education figures. District records drill by function, by object, and by funding source (SACS resource) — and each named funding source drills further into what it bought, by object — every level reconciling to the same gated figure. Unaudited actual expenditures as filed under the Standardized Account Code Structure; district comparison is per ADA, education's standard denominator. Annually, about seven months after the school fiscal year ends.
CSU campuses The California State University's audited systemwide financial statements (GAAP/GASB), campus by campus, plus the CSU Fact Book for enrollment. The 23 campuses and a visible systemwide & eliminations line reconcile to CSU's audited University total. Audited GAAP accrual — exact to the thousand, the finest resolution CSU publishes (a different, accurately-named tier from K-12's to-the-cent). NOT the state budget's enacted basis. Per enrolled student uses headcount, not FTES. Annually. This is the one layer that is NOT auto-reproducible: the CSU source is bot-gated, so refreshing it requires a manual browser download — stated plainly on the CSU page and in docs/SCOPE.md.
Community colleges The Chancellor's Office CCFS-311 public reporting portal — every district's Current Expense of Education (ECS 84362), the community-college analog of K-12's, for fifteen fiscal years — plus the SCFF apportionment Exhibit C and the MIS codes for the college roster. The 73 districts reconcile to the Chancellor's Office's own printed statewide total, every year. Modified-accrual on the CCC Budget and Accounting Manual (the same shape as K-12's SACS) — exact to the dollar, the resolution the portal publishes, and independently validated by each district's mandatory CPA audit (Ed Code 84040). NOT the state budget's enacted basis. The apportionment-derived figures — funded FTES, state general fund, per-FTES, community-supported status — reach only the five years with a readable Exhibit C (FY2018-19 through FY2023-24, FY2021-22 excepted), and not every fact in each; where a figure the source does not publish would appear, it is shown as not-published rather than derived. The college roster is the current MIS list and is not asserted about earlier years. Annually. Unlike CSU, this layer IS auto-reproducible: every source is a public endpoint the pipeline fetches without credentials (python3 pipeline/fetch_ccc_data.py --refresh).
UC campuses The University of California's own Annual Financial Report (audited by PwC) — its per-campus Campus Financial Facts table, whose ten campuses plus UC's own printed Systemwide column reconcile exactly to the audited total, for each of five fiscal years (FY2020-21 through FY2024-25) — plus the UCOP actual-FTE series for the denominator. Medical centers, auxiliary enterprises, and the DOE laboratory are stripped on UC's own published lines — shown separately, never deleted — because a raw UC figure is two-fifths hospital ledger. Audited GAAP accrual — exact to the thousand, the finest resolution UC publishes, with every campus column proven by a column-sum check. FY2019-20 is held, not shipped: its unaudited campus table misses the audited total by ~351K where every later year ties, so it is shown as a held, not-published point rather than published at a lower standard. UC publishes the DOE laboratory two ways across the window; the strip removes the same quantity either way, and that assembly break is stated where the five-year core line crosses it. The per-campus table is UC's auditor-read "other information," marked unaudited; the figure it reconciles to is audited. The strip's limit is stated on the face: hospitals are stripped, medical schools are not. NOT the state budget's enacted basis. Per-student uses student FTE, medical residents excluded on UC's own line. Annually. Auto-reproducible: both sources are public ucop.edu URLs the pipeline fetches without credentials (python3 pipeline/fetch_uc_data.py --refresh).
Special districts The State Controller's special-district filings and its published lists of late and missing filers. As filed — this is the one layer with no published control total to reconcile against, and every record, export, and citation says so on its face. Annually, with the Controller's filing cycle.

How far back each layer goes

Each layer covers the years its source supports at a resolution the gate can prove, and no further. Where a layer stops, it is because extending it would either fail the gate or quietly change what the figures mean — and each stopping point is a decision with a recorded reason, not an accident of what was loaded.

  • State budget and actuals — nine years (FY2017-18 through FY2025-26), the span the Department of Finance's structured budget API serves; earlier years return an empty result rather than an error. The newest year is enacted-only until its actuals publish.
  • Cities and counties — eight years (FY2016-17 through FY2023-24). They stop at FY2016-17 deliberately: the State Controller's expenditure taxonomy changed at FY2017, and before it, police and fire are not separable — both read simply "Public Safety" in the data. A series that cannot carry the per-service figures these pages are built on would be a different product, not a deeper one (the historical-depth finding).
  • K-12 schools — nine years (FY2016-17 through FY2024-25), each reconciling to CDE's Current Expense of Education to the cent.
  • Community colleges — fifteen years (FY2009-10 through FY2023-24) of Current Expense of Education, the portal's own coverage; the apportionment-derived figures reach only the five years with a readable Exhibit C (FY2018-19 through FY2023-24, FY2021-22 excepted).
  • CSU — one year (FY2023-24), and it cannot be extended: the audited-statement source at calstate.edu returns HTTP 403 to every scripted request, so the control total for any older year is uncomputable, not merely unreconciled. A year that cannot be gated does not ship.
  • UC — five years (FY2020-21 through FY2024-25); FY2019-20 is held, above.
  • Special districts — eight years (FY2016-17 through FY2023-24), as filed, at the pinned tier described below.

How every figure is verified

The Ledger's central discipline is a rule we call the reconciliation gate: before anything is published, every figure is recomputed from the raw source data, and the result must reproduce a total the source itself published. If any entity, in any year, fails that check, the pipeline refuses to write — the site keeps its previous data rather than publish a number that cannot be verified.

THE RECONCILIATION GATE, CONCRETELY Every state budget year's agency rows must sum to the statewide total the Department of Finance publishes beside them, exactly, in thousands. Every extracted year of actuals must reproduce Schedule 6's statewide control totals. Every city-year and county-year must reproduce the Controller's own published total for that government. Every school district, every year, must reproduce CDE's published Current Expense of Education figure — in practice, to the cent. Every community-college district's Current Expense of Education must sum to the Chancellor's Office's own printed statewide total, to the dollar, in each of fifteen years. UC's ten campuses plus UC's own printed Systemwide column must equal its audited total to the thousand — for each of five fiscal years, with every campus column proven by a column-sum check, and a sixth year (FY2019-20) held rather than shipped because it does not reconcile. Where the site drills below those figures — funds, programs, form lines, object detail — the children must sum exactly to the gated parent. And because a total can be right while its parts are mislabeled, a second family of checks (shape gates) verifies that the classification is sane: a real police department cannot vanish for a year, a catch-all bucket cannot silently absorb a city's spending.

A gate is only as good as what it is anchored to, and the anchors are not all the same kind of thing. Naming the difference matters more than a tidy claim of uniformity:

  • Reconciled to a published control. The state budget, the state actuals, cities, counties, K-12 schools, the community colleges, CSU and UC each reconcile to a figure the source itself published — DOF's statewide total, Schedule 6, the Controller's per-government totals, CDE's Current Expense of Education, the Chancellor's Office's printed Table VI, and the universities' audited statements. A mismatch means this record disagrees with the source, and nothing is published.
  • Pinned for tamper-evidence only. There is nothing here to reconcile against: no published control total exists for special districts, because the state does not publish one. That layer is published as filed, at a visibly different tier, with the caveat on every record rather than in a footnote. Its figures carry a recorded pin so that any later alteration of this record is caught, but a pin only says the file has not changed since it was built. It is not a claim that anyone has confirmed the figures are right.

The same care applies within a gated layer. For FY 2025-26 the Department of Finance's own published statewide total exceeds the sum of its own published agency rows by $1.638 million — about five ten-thousandths of one percent. That difference is inside the source's figures, not this record's. The Ledger reports DOF's agency rows as published rather than quietly adjusting them to close the gap, records the difference as an exact reviewed figure, and fails the build if it ever changes. Two limits are worth stating: that gate holds at agency level, because agencies carry items with no department attribution, so departments never sum to agencies in any year; and no statewide total can detect a transfer between two agencies, which leaves the total unchanged.

You can check the record yourself. Every data file carries a SHA-256 integrity digest, displayed on its page. Download the file, run python3 pipeline/verify_digest.py <file> from the repository, and the recomputed digest must match the displayed one. The pipelines that build each file are in the same repository, so any figure can be traced from the page back to the official source that produced it. A test suite re-runs the gates against the shipped files on every change — and the gates themselves are checked by mutation testing: every layer's figures are deliberately tampered with, one at a time, with the digest re-stamped to hide the edit. If the suite still passes, the test was reading the pipeline's own claims rather than the data, and it is rewritten until a tampered figure breaks it. That discipline began when exactly this weakness was found in the UC layer before it shipped.

When a figure changes

Published figures are not permanent. A source can restate what it published, redefine what a figure counts, or file late; and we can find an error in our own extraction. So every refresh compares the figures it is about to publish against the ones already published and records what moved, in the record of changes — including figures that appear and disappear, not only those whose value changes, because a reclassification moves money between categories that did not previously exist.

That record says what changed and by how much. It does not say why, and this is deliberate. A source restating its own figure and a source redefining what the figure means are indistinguishable from the outside — they differ only in the source's intent, which lives in its release notes rather than in its numbers. On the Controller's layers it is starker still: those pipelines ask the portal to aggregate the rows and return totals, so the underlying rows never reach us, and once the portal revises, the earlier figure cannot be reproduced by anyone, including us. Rather than guess at a cause, or ask a person to assert one on every refresh, the record reports only what it can show. One historical entry carries a cause, and it is labelled: our own FY 2016-17 city classifier fix, where the cause is known because it was our commit.

The record begins on the day it shipped, 19 July 2026, and says so on its face. Changes made before then were not captured, because the earlier figures were overwritten rather than compared.

What this site refuses to do

  • No ranking. Governments appear alphabetically, always. A per-resident figure is context, not a score; differences often reflect structure — which services a government provides, what its tax base looks like — rather than choices, and the notes on each record say when that is so.
  • No characterizing. The Ledger does not attach adjectives to numbers. There are no efficiency scores, no grades, no colors that pass judgment; direction is shown in ink, and a difference column is never described as good or bad. The test suite scans every page for characterizing language and fails if it appears.
  • No conclusions. The record answers what was budgeted, what was spent, and by whom — not whether it was wise. That question belongs to the reader.
  • No vendor data. We investigated publishing who the state pays and found that California does not publish vendor payment data that can be honestly reconciled to its budget — the state's own vendor files cover roughly a tenth of recorded spending, with no stable identifiers. Rather than publish precise-looking figures with unknowable gaps, we published the finding: the vendor-data investigation.
  • No sums across layers. State, county, city, school, and district figures overlap — about half of what counties and school districts report receiving is money the state budget already shows. Wherever layers meet on this site, the page says the figures do not add, with the measured overlap, and never shows a combined total.

Built to outlast its maker

Citizen Ledger is static files. There is no server, no database, no API key, no account, and no per-use cost anywhere in the system — a standing rule of the project, recorded in its scope document: any feature that would require one is out of scope by default. The exceptions are two keyless, free public services — map tiles and the Census Bureau's geocoder — and when either is unreachable, the record still works.

The rule is not a technical preference. When we surveyed every prior attempt at this work, the pattern was not competition but abandonment: the most serious California fiscal-transparency projects died when their builder's attention, funding, or staffing moved on. A site that is only files, rebuilt by pipelines anyone can run, has nothing that expires.

Known limits, stated openly

  • Special districts are as-filed, not reconciled. The state publishes no control total for them; the layer is labeled accordingly, everywhere.
  • Accounting bases differ by layer. Enacted appropriations are plans; Controller filings are retrospective actuals; SACS figures are unaudited. Each page states its basis, and the Ledger never compares figures across bases as if they were the same kind of number.
  • Actuals lag. State actuals for a fiscal year appear the following January; local filings take roughly a year. The current state fiscal year has enacted figures only, and its Actuals view says so rather than showing a blank.
  • Layers never sum. The same dollar can appear in the state budget, a county's revenues, and a school district's books. Every page where the layers meet carries the measured statement of that overlap.
  • Some context data is dated. The Controller's services checklist — which tells us when a city's police or fire service is provided by another government — was last published for FY 2015-16; arrangements may have changed, and every note that uses it says so.
  • Map boundaries are simplified for display and are never used to assign an address to a jurisdiction — that assignment comes from the Census Bureau's full-resolution geography.

Verifying authenticity

This site's authentic figures are the ones whose SHA-256 digests match those published here and reproduce from the official sources via the pipelines in the repository. That is how a reader tells the real Ledger from an altered copy: a fork or mirror whose data files cannot reproduce these digests — checked with python3 pipeline/verify_digest.py <file> — is not the authentic record, whatever it calls itself. The code and data below are free to reuse; the name is not: a fork may use both, but may not present itself as Citizen Ledger.

Open and reproducible

The data files, the pipelines that build them, the pages, the verification tools, and the investigation documents that decided what to publish and what to refuse are all in one public repository. The split is deliberate: the code is open source under the Apache License 2.0 — fork it, adapt it, build another state's ledger with it; the data is CC0, public domain — cite freely, no permission needed; the name is neither — "Citizen Ledger" is not licensed for reuse, so a fork cannot present its own figures as this record (the Apache license's trademark exclusion, stated again in the repository's NOTICE file). Every citation the site produces carries its source, its accounting basis, its generation date, and a permalink that reproduces the exact view. The method documents — including the findings on vendor data, special districts and schools, address lookup, drill-down depth, school funding sources, the funding-source × object cross-tab, the community-college gate, and the UC strip and gate — record not just what the site does but why, so the reasoning can be checked along with the arithmetic. A plain-language guide to reading the record sits above them, and what is deliberately unfinished, with the reasons, is kept current beside them. The security posture — what the static architecture eliminates, what risks remain, and how to report a problem — is documented in docs/SECURITY.md.
