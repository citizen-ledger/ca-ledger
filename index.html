Methodology

The Ledger records appropriations as enacted. It does not rank, characterize, or evaluate them. Every figure on this page comes from the official dataset described below and can be independently verified.

METHOD NOTES
M-1Scope is all state funds: General Fund, special funds, and bond funds, matching the enacted budget's Summary Charts. Federal pass-through dollars are excluded by default and shown via the "Federal funds" control. Per-resident figures use Department of Finance report E-4 population estimates for each fiscal year.
M-2Negative appropriations exist in the source data and are kept as published — they are offsets and transfers (for example, Statewide General Administrative Expenditures Pro Rata, and the Public School System Stabilization Account in 2021-22 through 2024-25), not errors. Rows carrying them are marked with a † method note.
M-3Department rows may not sum to the agency total: agency totals come from the source's agency-level table, while the department list is the set of rows the source publishes for web display. Where the gap exceeds rounding, the schedule says so.
M-4Agency groupings reflect the Department of Finance's current organizational mapping, which for earlier years can differ from the structure printed in that year's budget documents; statewide totals are unaffected.
M-8The K-12 schools page shows the same money this page's "K thru 12 Education" agency appropriates, seen from the receiving end — school districts' reported actuals, reconciled to CDE's published figures. About half of what schools report receiving is this page's K-12 money; the rest is chiefly local property tax and federal grants that never appear here. The two pages are never added; the schools page carries the full computed statement.
M-5The city view shows reported actual revenues and expenditures from cities' standardized State Controller filings — a different accounting basis from this page's enacted appropriations. Its police and fire service-provision notes derive from the State Controller's services checklist, whose most recent published vintage is FY 2015-16, and service arrangements may have changed since then. The supplementary flag on cities reporting under $5 per resident for a service is a heuristic backstop for the checklist's age, not a current-year survey of who provides the service.
M-6The Actuals view places actual expenditures beside enacted appropriations — both under the Budgetary-Legal basis, from the Department of Finance's own Schedule 9 ("Comparative Statement of Expenditures"). Actuals for a fiscal year first publish about six and a half months after it closes and may be restated in later publications; the vintage used is named on the view and in every citation. Extraction is verified against Schedule 6 statewide control totals before publication — a year that cannot be verified (currently FY 2020-21, whose source PDFs do not extract reliably) shows no actuals rather than approximate ones. The difference column reflects mid-year legislation, re-appropriations and carryover of multi-year authority, reversions, and fund reclassifications between publications; it is not a judgment of any kind, and actuals legitimately exceed enactment for some agencies.
SOURCES & RECORD
Primary source: the JSON API behind the California Department of Finance's official eBudget site — ebudget.ca.gov/api/publication/e/{year}/… — the same data that renders ebudget.ca.gov's Enacted Budget pages. Enacted figures are appropriations under California's Budgetary-Legal basis of accounting, fixed when each year's Budget Act is signed; they are not actual expenditures, and audited year-end results differ. One new fiscal year is added per annual Budget Act, typically signed in late June. The 2024-25 figures reproduce the published Summary Charts exactly ($297,862M).
RECORD INTEGRITY
Verify independently: download data.js and run python3 pipeline/verify_digest.py data.js from the repository — it recomputes the SHA-256 of the canonical JSON payload, which must match the digest above. A record you can check is a record you can trust.
Citation links reproduce the exact view — fiscal year, view, unit, and drill level — of the person citing. Dollar values are stated in billions and rounded to the nearest million, which is why some small items display as $0M.
