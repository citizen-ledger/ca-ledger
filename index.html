Methodology

What these figures are

Enacted appropriations under California's Budgetary-Legal basis of accounting: the spending plan fixed when each fiscal year's Budget Act is signed. Enacted figures do not change after publication. Dollar values are stated in billions and rounded to the nearest million, which is why some small items display as $0M.

What they are not

They are not actual expenditures — money as it is later spent — and they are not vendor-level payments. Mid-year revisions and audited year-end results differ from enacted figures. Year-to-year comparisons on this page are in nominal dollars, not adjusted for inflation or population; a per-resident figure is shown alongside each total.

Exact source and update cadence

The JSON API behind the Department of Finance's official eBudget site — https://ebudget.ca.gov/api/publication/e/{fiscal-year}/… — the same data that renders ebudget.ca.gov's Enacted Budget pages. General, special, and bond funds make up the state total, matching the enacted Summary Charts; federal pass-through funds are a user-controlled toggle; reimbursements and nongovernmental-cost funds are excluded, matching how budget documents present state spending. One new fiscal year is added per annual Budget Act, typically signed in late June. Population figures for per-resident amounts come from Department of Finance report E-4.

The city view

The city view shows reported actual revenues and expenditures from the standardized annual reports all California cities file with the State Controller (bythenumbers.sco.ca.gov) — a different accounting basis from this page's enacted appropriations, and its own page states it. One caveat is repeated here because it affects comparisons: the police and fire service-provision notes on that page derive from the State Controller's services checklist, whose most recent published vintage is FY 2015-16, and service arrangements may have changed since then. The supplementary flag on cities reporting under $5 per resident for a service is a heuristic backstop for the checklist's age, not a current-year survey of who provides the service.

Known caveats

  • Negative appropriations exist in the source data and are kept as published. Examples in the loaded years: Statewide General Administrative Expenditures (Pro Rata), between −$0.7B and −$1.0B General Fund each year; the Public School System Stabilization Account, −$2.6B to −$0.9B special funds in 2021-22 through 2024-25; the Office of Emergency Services, −$3.9B special funds in 2022-23; and, at agency level, Government Operations, −$563M special funds in 2022-23. These are offsets and transfers in the budget documents, not errors. They display as signed numbers; a department whose net amount is negative shows a zero-length bar.
  • The agency fund-mix strip omits fund classes with net-negative totals. When an agency's total for a fund class is zero or below, that class does not appear in the strip or its key, so the strip's segments show shares of the positive-fund subtotal rather than of the agency total printed above it.
  • Department rows may not sum to the agency total. Agency totals come from the source's agency-level table; the department list is the set of rows the source publishes for web display, which for some agencies covers less than the agency total.
  • Agency groupings reflect the source's current mapping. The API assigns departments to agencies using the Department of Finance's current organizational structure, which for earlier years can differ from the structure printed in that year's budget documents; statewide totals are unaffected.

Sources & method

Figures are enacted-budget expenditures: appropriations under California's Budgetary-Legal basis of accounting, as published by the Department of Finance at ebudget.ca.gov when each year's Budget Act was signed. They are the spending plan fixed at enactment, not audited actual spending; mid-year revisions and actuals differ. General, special, and bond funds make up the state total; federal pass-through funds are shown via the toggle; reimbursements and nongovernmental-cost funds are excluded, matching how budget documents present state spending. "Per resident" divides the displayed total by the Department of Finance population estimate (report E-4) for that fiscal year. A new enacted budget is published each June — run pipeline/fetch_state_data.py then; it rewrites data.js in place. City-level data (State Controller's Office annual reports) is live in the city view. The Methodology section above states what these figures are, what they are not, and the known caveats.
